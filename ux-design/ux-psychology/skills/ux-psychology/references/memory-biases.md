**One-liner:** Invite users to leave your app at the right moment
**Definition:** Exit points are meant to respect people’s time. They are opportunities to “put down” the product when users feel they have reached something. They are critical to an overall experience when you want to avoid product fatigue and . (Related to: )
**Product Example:** If you scroll through the TikTok feed regularly and for long periods, TikTok will eventually encourage you to leave the app, but the strength of their nudge is weak compared to the power of the variable reward that’s just one flick of the thumb away.
**Tip:** Apply Provide Exit Points thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.

## 🎢 Peak-End Rule
**One-liner:** People judge an experience by its peak and how it ends.
**Definition:** Users don’t merely evaluate an experience based on the average or a sum of all the micro-experiences. Instead, their brain heavily weighs the peaks (high or low) and the end of the experience. Peaks—when pleasant—often correspond to memorable sprinkled into the user journey.
**Product Example:** The last step of Adobe’s subscription cancellation flow isn’t clear. It pushes for more product usage while tucking away the confirmation message, which gives a bad last impression of their product: to “signal” their gratitude after a donation.
**Tip:** After you finish charging a Tesla, this redesigned interface provides one last reminder of what you just accomplished.
**Sources:** [Kahneman, Well-Being: The Foundations of Hedonic Psychology (1999)](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1999-02842-000)

## 👅 Sensory Appeal
**One-liner:** Users engage more with things appealing to multiple senses
**Definition:** Why are we tempted to eat sweets when walking by a bakery in the morning? The smell out of the oven is strong enough to make us stop. The sights, sounds, feels, tastes, and smells of products are designed to engage users’ senses. And when multiple senses are engaged, people are more likely to create an emotional connection with the brand.
**Tip:** Apply Sensory Appeal thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.

## 🧩 Zeigarnik Effect
**One-liner:** People remember incomplete tasks better than completed ones
**Definition:** Lewin’s field theory states that a task in progress creates task-specific tension. This tension is relieved when the task is completed, but if the task is interrupted, it stays. That tension makes relevant information more accessible and more easily remembered.
**Tip:** Apply Zeigarnik Effect thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.
**Sources:** [The Zeigarnik Effect: Why it is so hard to leave things incomplete, Abhishek Chakraborty (2017)](https://medium.com/@coffeeandjunk/design-psychology-zeigarnik-effect-a53688b7f6d1), [Zeigarnik Effect Interruptions (2020)](https://www.psychologistworld.com/memory/zeigarnik-effect-interruptions-memory), [Untersuchungen zur Handlungs- und Affektpsychologie. III. Zeigarnik, B. Das Behalten erledigter und unerledigter Handlungen](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1927-02365-001)

## 🧤 Endowment Effect
**One-liner:** Users value something more if they feel it's theirs
**Definition:** Users are more likely to want to keep something that they own than acquire that same thing when they don’t own it. They tend to overvalue the things they own, regardless of their objective market value. (Related: )
**Product Example:** Blinkist’s onboarding gets users to save book preferences to their library to encourage users stick around until the very end.
**Tip:** Apply Endowment Effect thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.

## 🛍 Chunking
**One-liner:** People remember grouped information better
**Definition:** In cognitive psychology, chunking is when individual pieces of an information set are broken down and grouped in a meaningful whole. These chunks bypass the limited working memory capacity (see Miller’s Law) and allow the working memory to be more efficient. Chunks are easier to retrieve because your brain creates higher-order cognitive representations of the items within each chunk.

In terms of interface design, it comes down to breaking down steps, text, and interface elements into smaller pieces so that users can process, understand, and remember information them better.
**Product Example:** The page you’re currently reading contains 100+ psychological principles. That’s hard to remember. To help you memorize these principles, we grouped them in 4 categories (chunks):
**Tip:** Stripe is a large company with dozens of products and use cases. But Stripe’s main navigation only has 5 simple links and their products are chunked into 3 simple categories, organized around their users’ main jobs-to-be-done:
**Sources:** [Chunking, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chunking_(psychology)), [How Chunking Helps Content Processing, NN/g](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/chunking/)

## 💚 Delighters
**One-liner:** People remember more unexpected and playful pleasures
**Definition:** We remember and respond favorably to small, unexpected, and playful pleasures. But, for your delighters to have a positive effect, you must first meet or exceed the user’s basic expectations. Otherwise, that moment will likely fall flat. Also, over time, delighters become expected features losing that slight edge.

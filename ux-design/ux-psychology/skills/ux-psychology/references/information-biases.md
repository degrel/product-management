## 👀 Hick's Law
**One-liner:** More options leads to harder decisions
**Definition:** Hick’s Law predicts that the time and the effort it takes to make a decision, increases with the number of options. The more choices, the more time users take to make their decisions.
**Tip:** Find an area where you have a lot of options or a lot of repetitions.
**Sources:** [make sure the items are familiar;](https://www.nngroup.com/videos/hicks-law-long-menus/), [Redefining Hick’s Law, Smashing Magazine (2020)](https://www.smashingmagazine.com/2012/02/redefining-hicks-law/), [Page Conversion Rate Optimization, Instapage (2020)](https://instapage.com/blog/landing-page-conversion-rate-optimization)

## 💼 Confirmation Bias
**One-liner:** People look for evidence that confirms what they think
**Definition:** People tend to search for, interpret, prefer, and recall information in a way that reinforces their personal beliefs or hypotheses.
**Product Example:** Facebook optimizes for what users want to see and creates “filter bubbles”. Their feed shows you more of the information that confirms what you believe in (whether that’s true or not):
**Tip:** Apply Confirmation Bias thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.

## 👁 Priming
**One-liner:** Previous stimuli influence users' decision
**Definition:** Subtle visual or verbal suggestions help users recall specific information, influencing how they respond. Priming works by activating an association or representation in users short-term memory just before another stimulus or task is introduced.
**Product Example:** Brave primes you to focus on the importance of privacy during their onboarding, making you more likely to choose DuckDuckGo instead of Google search. Brave can hence subtly (but respectfully) reduce the brand awareness of their number one competitor: Google.
**Tip:** Apply Priming thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.
**Sources:** [Priming and the science behind onboarding, Mind the product (2019)](https://www.mindtheproduct.com/priming-and-the-science-behind-onboarding/), [Priming Psychology, Wikipedia (2020)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Priming_(psychology))

## 🚛 Cognitive Load
**One-liner:** Total amount of mental effort that is required to complete a task
**Definition:** Cognitive load is the total amount of mental effort that is required to complete a task. You can think of it as the processing power needed by the user to interact with a product. If the information that needs to be processed exceeds the user’s ability to handle it, the cognitive load is too high.
**Tip:** Apply Cognitive Load thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.
**Sources:** [Cognitive load, Steve Krug (2019)](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/18197267-don-t-make-me-think-revisited), [Minimize Cognitive Load to Maximize Usability, NNGroup (2013)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/minimize-cognitive-load/)

## ⚓️ Anchoring Bias
**One-liner:** Users rely heavily on the first piece of information they see
**Definition:** The initial information that users get affects subsequent judgments. Anchoring often works even when the nature of the anchor doesn’t have any relation with the decision at hand. It’s useful to increase perceived value.
**Product Example:** Brick and mortar shops often display very expensive items in the front with visible price tags so that the item you end up seeking seems cheaper.
**Tip:** Apply Anchoring Bias thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.
**Sources:** [Not all anchors are created equal, Journal of Economic Psychology (2013)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0167487013000834), [Predictably Irrational, Dan Ariely (2008)](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/1713426.Predictably_Irrational)

## 🍰 Progressive Disclosure
**One-liner:** Users are less overwhelmed if they're exposed to complex features later
**Definition:** An interface is easier to use when complex features are gradually revealed later. During the onboarding, show only the core features of your product, and as users get familiar, unveil new options. It keeps the interface simple for new users and progressively brings power to advanced users.
**Tip:** Apply Progressive Disclosure thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.
**Sources:** [Progressive Disclosure, NN Group (2006)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/progressive-disclosure/), [Designing for Progressive Disclosure, Gabriella Campagna (2019)](https://blog.prototypr.io/designing-for-progressive-disclosure-aabb5ddfbab4)

## 🎯 Fitts's Law
**One-liner:** Large and close elements are easier to interact with
**Definition:** Fitts’s law is a predictive model which states that the time to acquire a target is a function of the to and of the target. This is mainly used to model the act of pointing, either physically (e.g., with a hand) or virtually (e.g., with a computer mouse).
**Product Example:** Adobe uses a tiny subscription management link in their auto-renewal email to discourage people from cancelling their subscription. This is a common dark pattern unfortunately.
**Tip:** Apply Fitts's Law thoughtfully in your design. Test with real users to validate the effect.
**Sources:** [Fitts’s Law, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitts%27s_law), [Fitts’s Law, NN/G](https://www.nngroup.com/videos/fittss-law/)

## 🕶 Banner Blindness
**One-liner:** Users tune out the stuff they get repeatedly exposed to
**Definition:** Users have learned to ignore content that resembles ads, is close to ads, or appears in locations traditionally dedicated to ads.
**Tip:** Is your important information in the usual location of ads? Is it close to ads? Does it look like ads? If yes, then there are some good chances, it’ll be blocked by people’s brain.
**Sources:** [Banner Blindness Revisited - NNGroup (2018)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/banner-blindness-old-and-new-findings/)

## 🕺 Decoy Effect
**One-liner:** Create a new option that's easy to discard
**Definition:** When we are choosing between two alternatives, the addition of a third, less attractive option (the decoy) can influence our perception of the original two choices. Decoys are “asymmetrically dominated”: they are completely inferior to one option (the target) but only partially inferior to the other (the competitor). For this reason, the decoy effect is sometimes called the “asymmetric dominance effect.”
**Product Example:** The Economist subscription page ran a successful decoy a few years ago. They offered three subscriptions at the following prices:
**Tip:** The decoy was the “Print only” plan at the same price as the “Print + Web” plan, and most people bought the latter!
**Sources:** [Decoy Effect - The Decision Lab](https://thedecisionlab.com/biases/decoy-effect/), [The decoy effect: Why you make irrational choices every day (without even knowing it) - Kent Hendricks (2018)](https://kenthendricks.com/decoy-effect/)

## 🖼 Framing
**One-liner:** The way information is presented affects how users make decisions
**Definition:** The framing effect happens when your decision is influenced more by how the information is presented (or worded) than by the information itself. It’s partly due to the fact that people evaluate their losses and acquire insight in an asymmetric fashion (see and Prospect Theory, by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky).
**Product Example:** Blinkist could anchor the status quo (reading without Blinkist) as very time-consuming helps users see the value of their product and frame the main benefit of their app (saving time)
**Tip:** Tesla’s charging interface highlights the costs, but not the benefits of being electric. Framing the charging experience around that benefit (while still displaying the costs for transparency) could have addressed this issue:
**Sources:** [The Framing Effect, UXDesign (2019)](https://uxdesign.cc/the-framing-effect-in-ux-d77df34369ec), [Framing, SimplyPsychology (2022)](https://www.simplypsychology.org/framing-effect.html), [Thinking, Fast and Slow, by Daniel Kahneman (2014)](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/11468377-thinking-fast-and-slow)
